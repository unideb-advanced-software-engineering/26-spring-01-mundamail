---
title: 'ADR-002: Kafka as the Event Broker'
description: The decision made for the event handler
---

- Status: Active

---

## In the context of
- the Government Messaging System's event-driven layer, responsible for asynchronous message delivery, audit event streaming, and archival fan-out
- the decision to adopt an event broker as established in ADR-001

---

## facing the need for
- sustained throughput of approximately 83 messages per second to deliver 50,000 messages within 10 minutes (ASR-004)
- a durable, replayable event log that survives consumer failures without data loss, supporting the RTO/RPO requirements (ASR-001, ASR-002)
- a tamper-evident, append-only record of all system events serving as the primary audit trail for 12-month compliance and 5-year archival (ASR-010, ASR-015)
- the ability to add consumer groups (delivery workers, audit writers, archive writers) without modifying producers, keeping the system extensible
- decoupling of message production rate from consumption rate to absorb traffic spikes from up to 10,000 concurrent users (ASR-005)

---

## we decided for
- Apache Kafka as the event broker, with:
  - a 'message.send.requested' topic partitioned to support parallel consumer groups for delivery, audit, and archival
  - a 'msg.dlq' dead letter queue topic for blocked attachments and failed deliveries
  - consumer group isolation so delivery workers, audit writers, and archive writers each progress independently
  - log retention configured at 12 months for active topics and cold-tier offloading for 5-year archival compliance

---

## achieving
- linear horizontal scalability of message throughput by adding partitions and consumer instances, directly satisfying the 50,000-recipient mass messaging requirement (ASR-004)
- fire-and-forget publishing from the modular monolith, returning 202 to the caller in single-digit milliseconds and keeping the API response well within the 2-second SLA (ASR-003)
- automatic replay from committed offsets on consumer failure, removing the need for application-level retry logic and supporting the RPO of 1 hour (ASR-002)
- an immutable, append-only log that constitutes the tamper-evident audit trail, with retention policy as the sole mechanism for satisfying both 12-month and 5-year compliance requirements (ASR-010, ASR-015)
- traffic spike absorption — the Kafka log buffers bursts from 10,000 concurrent users without back-pressure reaching the API layer, preventing thread starvation in the monolith (ASR-005)
- a dead letter queue that captures blocked attachment events and failed deliveries without stalling the main delivery pipeline, satisfying BR-MM-006 with full admin visibility (ASR-016)

---

## accepting that (we will have)
- non-trivial operational overhead in managing a Kafka cluster, including partition rebalancing, consumer lag monitoring, and broker health
- eventual consistency between event production and consumer processing — there is no guarantee of delivery within a specific sub-second window after the event is published
- the need for explicit idempotency handling in consumers, as Kafka's at-least-once delivery semantics can result in duplicate events during rebalancing or retry
- schema management discipline required across all topics — consumer updates must be coordinated when event schemas evolve to avoid deserialization failures
